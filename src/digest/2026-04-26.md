### [Anthropic Built a Test Marketplace for Agent-on-Agent Commerce](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/25/anthropic-created-a-test-marketplace-for-agent-on-agent-commerce/)

Anthropic ran a classified experiment where AI agents played both buyer and seller roles, completing real transactions for real goods with real money — a live proof-of-concept for autonomous agent commerce.

> The interesting design question here is trust and verification between agents. If agents can transact autonomously, the next hard problem is establishing shared contracts, escrow mechanisms, and audit trails. Enterprise deployments will need serious governance layers before anything like this goes to production.

### [DeepSeek V4 Pro (1.6T) and Flash — MIT License, 1M Context](https://www.latent.space/p/ainews-deepseek-v4-pro-16t-a49b-and)

DeepSeek released V4-Pro (1.6T parameter MoE, 49B active, 32T training tokens, FP4) and V4-Flash (284B, 13B active), both with 1M token context via new Compressed Sparse Attention techniques. MIT licensed, both Base and Instruct variants available. Now the largest open-weights model available, ahead of Kimi K2.6.

> The combination of MIT license, frontier-adjacent performance, and 1M context is a serious enterprise option — especially for on-premises or sovereign deployments where routing to a US API is off the table. Worth evaluating for internal enterprise search and document processing pipelines.

### [GPT-5.5 Is Out — OpenAI Says Start Your Prompts From Scratch](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/25/gpt-5-5-prompting-guide/#atom-everything)

GPT-5.5 is now in the API. OpenAI's explicit guidance: treat it as a new model family, start from the smallest prompt that preserves your product contract, then tune reasoning effort, verbosity, tool descriptions, and output format. They also recommend sending a short user-visible status message before multi-step tool calls.

> The "start from scratch" advice is worth taking seriously. Prompt stacks tuned for GPT-5.2 or 5.4 will underperform on 5.5 if you just swap the model string. Budget prompt re-evaluation time before rolling 5.5 into any production system — the verbosity and reasoning defaults have shifted meaningfully.

### [OpenAI Unifies Codex Into GPT-5.5 — No Separate Coding Model](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/25/romain-huet/#atom-everything)

OpenAI confirmed there will be no GPT-5.5-Codex. Since GPT-5.4, the Codex line has been merged into the main model. GPT-5.5 brings "strong gains in agentic coding, computer use, and any task on a computer."

> One model family to rule the coding domain. This simplifies procurement decisions but removes the option for a specialized, potentially cheaper coding model — everything routes through the flagship tier. For high-volume code generation use cases, the cost math will need a fresh look.

### [Cohere Merges With Aleph Alpha — European Sovereign AI Play](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/25/why-cohere-is-merging-with-aleph-alpha/)

Canadian AI startup Cohere is taking over Germany-based Aleph Alpha, backed by Schwarz Group (Lidl's parent). Both companies and their governments intend to offer a sovereign AI alternative for European enterprises in a landscape dominated by US providers.

> Digital sovereignty is a real procurement driver in regulated industries outside the US — financial services, healthcare, government. Worth watching as the enterprise LLM market fragments by geography.

### [The People Do Not Yearn for Automation](https://www.theverge.com/podcast/917029/software-brain-ai-backlash-databases-automation)

Nilay Patel's essay explores why AI is unpopular with the general public even as ChatGPT usage keeps climbing. His argument: people with "software brain" — who model the world as data flows to be automated — are becoming detached from everyone else. Regular people don't see writing code as an opportunity. They feel flattened by AI.

> This is the adoption gap enterprise AI architects don't talk about enough. You can build technically excellent agentic systems and still have adoption fail because people feel depersonalized by them. Designing AI products that people actually trust and want is a different problem than building them — and it starts much earlier, at the framing of what the product is trying to do to a human.

</div>
