### [Anthropic's Claude Mythos Is Too Dangerous to Release — Restricted to 40 Cybersecurity Partners](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/07/anthropic-mythos-ai-model-preview-security/)

Anthropic unveiled Claude Mythos Preview under "Project Glasswing" — a restricted access program rather than a GA release. The model autonomously discovered thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities across every major OS and web browser, including a 27-year-old OpenBSD kernel bug. Researchers report it chained 4–5 separate vulnerabilities into working exploits at a success rate orders of magnitude above prior models.

> This is a threshold moment. When a model can autonomously write browser exploits chaining four vulnerabilities with JIT heap sprays, the security posture of every enterprise running internet-connected systems changes overnight. The fact that Anthropic is withholding it and investing $100M in coordinated defensive work signals they think it's real — and that the race between offense and defense is now model-driven. Enterprise architects need to think about how AI-assisted vulnerability discovery changes their threat model before this capability is more broadly accessible.

Anthropic's revenue surged from $19B ARR in March to $30B ARR in April — a 15x run-rate increase year-over-year. Alongside the Mythos announcement, Anthropic expanded its TPU deal with Google and Broadcom to meet demand. Some analysts project Anthropic could exceed $90B ARR by end of 2026.

For enterprise buyers, this validates Anthropic's staying power and makes the Claude ecosystem a safe long-term bet. The expanded TPU deal also matters: it's a signal that Anthropic is betting on Google's silicon roadmap, not just Azure.

### [OpenAI's Harness Engineering: 1M LOC, 1B Tokens/Day, 0% Human Code](https://www.latent.space/p/harness-eng)

Ryan Lopopolo from OpenAI published a detailed essay on "Harness Engineering" — the discipline of building the scaffolding, evaluation frameworks, and orchestration infrastructure that lets AI agents produce code at scale. OpenAI's internal system generates 1M+ lines of code and 1B tokens per day with zero human-written code and zero human code review.

> This is what fully agentic software development looks like in practice. The essay is dense and worth reading carefully — it's less about the AI itself and more about the infrastructure layer that makes sustained autonomous coding reliable. The takeaway for enterprise: the investment isn't just in the model, it's in the harness. Context engineering, eval design, and feedback loops are the real differentiators. "Vibe coding" doesn't scale; harness engineering does.

### [AgentArmor: Open-Source 8-Layer Security Framework for AI Agents](https://github.com/Agastya910/agentarmor)

AgentArmor is an open-source framework that wraps agentic architectures with 8 independent security layers targeting distinct attack surfaces: prompt injection detection, encrypted vector DB storage, instruction-data separation, action risk scoring, network egress control, PII redaction, inter-agent authentication, and just-in-time identity/permissions. Tested against the full OWASP ASI (Agentic Security Integrity) spec from December 2025.

> Most production agentic systems today have no real security boundary beyond "we trust the LLM." AgentArmor's action risk scoring layer (READ=1 → ADMIN=10) and human approval gates are particularly practical for enterprise deployments where agents touch sensitive data or take real-world actions. As we build agentic workflows internally, this kind of layered approach should be the baseline expectation, not an afterthought.

### [Microsoft Copilot Is "For Entertainment Purposes Only" Per Its Terms of Service](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/05/copilot-is-for-entertainment-purposes-only-according-to-microsofts-terms-of-service/)

Microsoft's terms of use for Copilot explicitly describe the product as "for entertainment purposes only," warning users not to rely on outputs for professional decisions. The language has circulated among enterprise risk and compliance teams as AI adoption accelerates in regulated industries.

> This is the kind of CYA language that creates real friction in enterprise AI governance. On one hand, every AI vendor has similar disclaimers. On the other, "entertainment only" sits awkwardly alongside Microsoft's aggressive Copilot for M365 sales push into healthcare, finance, and legal. For enterprise architects rolling out Copilot at scale, this ToS language needs to be part of the governance conversation — what policies govern AI-assisted decisions, and who owns accountability when outputs are wrong?

### [OpenAI's Vision for the AI Economy: Robot Taxes, Public Wealth Funds, 4-Day Workweek](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/06/openais-vision-for-the-ai-economy-public-wealth-funds-robot-taxes-and-a-four-day-work-week/)

OpenAI published a policy paper proposing responses to AI-driven economic disruption: taxes on AI profits redistributed through public wealth funds, expanded safety nets, and a four-day work week. The framing blends redistribution with continued AI development.

> OpenAI writing AI economic policy proposals is an odd posture from a company driving the displacement they're theorizing about. That said, the underlying questions are real — tax fintech and enterprise AI deployments enough to fund retraining programs? The four-day workweek angle reads more as thought leadership than near-term policy. What's more interesting architecturally: the implied assumption that AI productivity gains will be large enough and fast enough to require *structural* labor market intervention within the decade.
