### [Is Claude Code going to cost $100/month? Probably not — it's all very confusing](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/22/claude-code-confusion/)

Anthropic silently updated their pricing page to remove Claude Code from the $20/month Pro plan, restricting it to $100–$200/month Max plans. The change sparked immediate backlash on Reddit, HN, and Twitter. Anthropic's Head of Growth said it was a "~2% test," and the change was reverted within hours — but with zero official announcement at any point.

> The real damage isn't the pricing change itself — it's the communication failure. Enterprise teams making platform bets on Claude Code now have a trust gap. If Anthropic is testing $100/month as a floor, that's a five-fold increase overnight with no warning. Any architecture decision that bakes in a single provider's tooling should factor in that kind of risk. The reversal is good, but the pattern is something to watch.

### [GitHub Copilot tightens individual plans, cites agentic compute demands](https://github.blog/news-insights/company-news/changes-to-github-copilot-individual-plans/)

GitHub is pausing signups for individual Copilot plans, restricting Claude Opus 4.7 to the $39/month Pro+ tier, and dropping older Opus models. The company cited agentic workflows as the driver: long-running parallelized sessions now consume far more compute than the original plan structure was built for.

> Copilot built their pricing on a per-request model when agents were novelties. Now agents are the product, and per-request math doesn't survive a 10x token burn. This is the first major pricing crack that's directly attributable to agentic workloads — and every enterprise procurement team building AI cost models should update their projections accordingly. The era of flat-rate agentic AI was brief.

### [Mozilla used Claude Mythos to find 271 Firefox vulnerabilities](https://blog.mozilla.org/en/privacy-security/ai-security-zero-day-vulnerabilities/)

Mozilla partnered with Anthropic and applied an early Claude Mythos Preview to Firefox's codebase. The result: 271 vulnerabilities identified and fixed in Firefox 150. Firefox CTO Bobby Holley called it a turning point — "defenders finally have a chance to win, decisively."

> This is a bigger deal than the headline number. AI-assisted offensive security research has been progressing fast; AI-assisted defense lagged behind. Firefox showing 271 real fixes from a single model pass on a mature, heavily-reviewed codebase is a signal that the asymmetry is shifting. Enterprises running large codebases should be watching this — the question is no longer whether AI security tooling works, but when it becomes a standard part of the SDLC.

### [OpenAI launches GPT-Image-2 with Thinking variants](https://openai.com/index/introducing-chatgpt-images-2-0/)

OpenAI's new image generation model is live in API and ChatGPT. GPT-Image-2 comes in thinking and non-thinking variants and shows a significant jump in text-rendering accuracy — the demo of precise, consistent text in generated images is the most technically impressive part.

> Multimodal generation has moved from "interesting demo" to genuinely useful for document-heavy workflows. Accurate in-image text opens up use cases in financial services — statements, reports, infographics — where earlier models were unreliable. The thinking variant is the one to watch for enterprise content pipelines that need predictable, structured output.

### [SpaceX working with Cursor, holds $60B acquisition option](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/21/spacex-is-working-with-cursor-and-has-an-option-to-buy-the-startup-for-60-billion/)

SpaceX has a $10B engineering contract with Cursor and an option to acquire the coding assistant startup for $60B. The deal pairs SpaceX's engineering scale with Cursor's developer tooling — but also exposes that neither Cursor nor xAI has proprietary frontier models competitive with Anthropic or OpenAI.

> Sixty billion for a coding IDE is the number that will make people stop and blink. But the underlying dynamic is straightforward: SpaceX wants to control the toolchain their engineers live in, and xAI wants distribution. The model dependency problem is the real story — Cursor is brilliant software built on someone else's intelligence. That's a durable acquisition risk that $60B doesn't fix.

### [NeoCognition raises $40M seed for agents that learn like humans](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/21/ai-research-lab-neocognition-lands-40m-seed-to-build-agents-that-learn-like-humans/)

Founded by an OSU researcher, NeoCognition is developing AI agents designed to become domain experts through continuous learning — not just retrieval or fine-tuning. The $40M seed round is one of the largest at that stage for an AI research lab.

> The "agents that learn" framing is doing a lot of work here, but the core idea — agents that accumulate genuine domain expertise over time rather than re-querying a static knowledge base — is exactly where enterprise agentic AI needs to go. Current RAG-based agents are good at retrieval; they're not good at getting better at a job. If NeoCognition ships what they're describing, it changes the architecture conversation significantly.
