### [Microsoft's new MAI models](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/2/microsofts-new-models/)
Simon Willison highlighted Microsoft's June 2 launch of MAI-Thinking-1, a reasoning model with 1T parameters and 35B active, and MAI-Code-1-Flash, a 137B-parameter code model with 5B active that is rolling into GitHub Copilot in VS Code. He also noted the updated paper shows the models were trained on Microsoft's own web crawl plus Common Crawl.
> Microsoft is building a fuller stack here: model, tuning story, and product distribution. That matters more to enterprise teams than one benchmark win, because it points to faster rollout across Copilot, Azure, and internal business workflows.

### [New Microsoft tool lets devs spin up AI behavior tests using text descriptions](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/02/new-microsoft-tool-lets-devs-spin-up-ai-behavior-tests-using-text-descriptions/)
TechCrunch reported on June 2 that Microsoft open-sourced ASSERT, a framework that turns natural-language policies and expected behaviors into scored tests for AI systems. It can generate scenarios, run them against the target system, and capture intermediate tool calls so teams can inspect where failures happen.
> This is the eval layer enterprise teams have been missing. Product rules, compliance language, and security constraints can become repeatable tests instead of living as scattered prompt text and manual QA.

### [Microsoft offers devs a better way to control AI agent behavior](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/02/microsoft-offers-devs-a-better-way-to-control-ai-agent-behavior/)
Microsoft introduced Agent Control Specification, an open source standard for defining what agents may do, what requires human approval, and what evidence must be logged. TechCrunch says ACS can intercept inputs, tool calls, tool outputs, and final responses, with SDK plug-ins for major agent stacks.
> Governance is moving into policy files and SDK hooks. If ACS sticks, architects get a cleaner way to carry security and compliance rules across mixed agent frameworks without rebuilding the same controls over and over.
</div>

### [Uber caps employee AI spending after blowing through budget in 4 months](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/02/uber-caps-employee-ai-spending-after-blowing-through-budget-in-four-months/)
Uber has placed a $1,500 monthly cap per employee per agentic coding tool after burning through its 2026 AI budget in four months. The company still allows exceptions with approval, following an earlier push that encouraged heavy internal AI usage.
> Token spend is now an architecture problem. Teams need routing, quotas, telemetry, and cost controls around agents the same way they already do for cloud resources.

### [Coralogix raises $200M on bet that someone needs to watch the AI agents](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/03/coralogix-raises-200m-in-race-to-build-the-monitoring-layer-for-ai-agents/)
Coralogix raised a $200 million Series F at a $1.6 billion post-money valuation to expand observability for AI-driven systems. The company says more than half of its enterprise customers already use its AI agent, or their own models through command-line and agentic interfaces, to investigate incidents and query operational data.
> This lines up with where production AI is heading. Once agents touch real systems, observability stops being plumbing and becomes part of the AI control plane.

### [Meta’s AI agent for WhatsApp Business is now available globally](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/03/metas-ai-agent-for-whatsapp-business-is-now-available-globally/)
Meta rolled out Meta Business Agent globally in WhatsApp Business and into Instagram DMs, with support for answering questions, recommending products, booking appointments, and escalating to humans. Larger businesses will pay for usage based on token consumption.
> This is one of the clearest signs that agent deployment is moving into existing business channels. Messaging already carries customer intent, so the real question is how well these agents hand off, log activity, and stay inside business policy.
</div>
